Overall response: the review is accepted in substance. Its central reframing — an auditable deterministic quant platform with experimental LLM forecasters, rather than an autonomous multi-agent desk — is adopted as the organizing premise of v2, stated in the revision note and enforced by the new authority rule ("no LLM in the mandatory path for closing, reducing, cancelling, reconciling, or protecting a position"). ## Adopted in full

**Finding 1 (P0) — Advisor edge validation.** v1 had no definition of edge; this was its worst gap. v2 §6 adds the Advisor Research Contract verbatim in spirit (pre-registered target, horizon, benchmark, metrics, `minimum_sample_size: 200`, thresholds written before data is seen), the baseline comparisons (always-flat, buy-and-hold, random-with-matched-frequency, deterministic same-features strategy), block-bootstrap confidence intervals, multiple-testing correction across advisors/prompt variants, Brier/reliability-curve calibration replacing self-declared conviction, and the economic-significance-after-costs requirement. A deterministic baseline strategy (vol-scaled momentum) is promoted to a first-class v1 component so the benchmark actually exists.

**Finding 2 (P0) — LLM replay contamination.** v1's claim that journaling gives backtesting "for free" is explicitly withdrawn in v2 §6. The three evaluation methods are now strictly separated: classical backtest (deterministic layers only), prospective shadow forecasts (the sole promotion-grade evidence for LLM advisors), and historical LLM replay (permanently labeled exploratory/contaminated). The full model-provenance record (provider, model id/version, prompt hashes, sampling params, cutoff and request/response timestamps, raw response hash, parser version) is required on every LLM invocation (§5).

**Finding 3 (P0) — Tax statement.** The "as a US SSN holder you likely have US tax reporting obligations" sentence is removed and replaced in §1 with the reviewer's recommended wording (residency determined by citizenship / green-card / substantial-presence tests, Taiwan assessed separately, cross-border professional advice before live deployment). The unconditional part — journal every fill in export-friendly form from day one — is retained since it is correct regardless of status.

**Finding 4 (P0) — Venue eligibility as a deployment gate.** v2 §1 adds the Venue Admission Checklist and a startup compliance preflight that queries official geoblock/account endpoints from the actual execution host and refuses to trade on ineligibility. Polymarket is demoted to research-only (no execution adapter in v1); Kalshi is out of scope until verified. The v1 claims "Hyperliquid is the strongest single choice" and "your MetaMask works there" are softened to "leading candidate, contingent on the checklist," including verification of the agent-wallet permission/revocation model against current official docs.

**Finding 5 (P0) — LLM risk approval as required gate.** `require_llm_risk_approval: true` is deleted. v2 §9 adopts the four-level authority hierarchy and the `llm_risk_critic` config block exactly as recommended (exposure-increasing actions only, 15s timeout, `failure_policy: reject_new_exposure`, may never block exits, increase size, or widen stops). The risk-manager prompt (§7) is rewritten from "final decision power" to "critic on new exposure only." The v1 initial-preferences framing of the risk manager "holding the power to make final decisions" is thereby reinterpreted: final authority is the deterministic risk engine.

**Finding 6 (P0) — Initial limits too permissive.** The reviewer's v1 limit set is adopted essentially verbatim (§9): 30% gross, 20% net directional, 10% single position, 1× leverage, 0.75/1.25% daily soft/hard halts, 2.5% weekly, 4% drawdown kill, order size as % of equity (fixing the $500-order vs. $200–500-account contradiction), human restart after hard halts. Soft/hard/kill are specified as explicit state transitions with behavior, not YAML comments. Limit raises are tied to operational criteria, not P&L.

**Finding 7 (P0) — Order schema and execution state machine.** v2 §5 adopts the `OrderIntent` schema (venue, account, instrument type, quantity + explicit unit, order type, TIF, reduce-only, expiry, snapshot/verdict provenance) with `Decimal` for all money/quantities, and §10 adopts the full durable state machine including deterministic client order IDs, at-most-once intent consumption via transactional outbox, idempotent retry, UNKNOWN recovery by querying venue truth before resubmission, partial-fill policy, cancel/replace race handling, continuous reconciliation as a P0 trigger, and tick/lot normalization.

**Finding 9 (P1) — Data governance.** v2 §3 adopts the full lineage field set (event/published/provider/ingested/available-to-strategy times, revision, payload hash, normalizer version, quality flags), UTC everywhere, sequence-gap detection, immutable raw landing zone, point-in-time symbol metadata, cross-source sanity checks with degraded mode, canonical-event news dedup, prompt-boundary escaping, and licensing review.

**Finding 10 (P1) — Don't mix instrument classes in v1.** Adopted: one venue, BTC+ETH only (SOL later), ≤1× effective exposure, prediction markets research-only with a future dedicated `PredictionOrderIntent` and separate risk engine, equities deferred (§2).

**Finding 11 (P1) — Self-conditioning on last three outcomes.** Adopted. v1's unsupported claim that it "measurably reduces overconfident repetition" is withdrawn. Advisors now receive a deterministic long-window calibration summary (coverage, hit rate, Brier, bucket calibration, regime breakdown, status/multiplier) with an explicit prompt instruction not to strategy-chase streaks; degradation acts through predeclared rules in the calibration layer (size multipliers, demotion to shadow), never through the LLM's own reaction to recent P&L (§7).

**Finding 13 (P1) — Protective orders.** Adopted (§9): documented trigger reference, no atomicity assumption without venue documentation, protection verified after every fill and partial fill, an independent read-only unprotected-position watchdog with a bounded time-to-flatten, risk computed from executable loss under stress scenarios rather than stop distance, liquidation-distance constraint retained even at 1×. ATR stop bounds survive only as sanity checks, explicitly not as the risk measure.

**Finding 14 (P1) — Paper/testnet criteria.** Adopted: shadow execution against live production books with conservative fill/impact/latency/adverse-selection modeling becomes the primary pre-live environment; testnet is repurposed for API mechanics and failure drills only; the measurable promotion thresholds (≥99.9% loop completion, 0 duplicates, 0 unprotected, 100% reconciliation, latency and slippage-error bounds, minimum event counts) replace v1's "tracking error is explainable" (§11, Gate C).

**Finding 16 (P1) — Observability and incident response.** Adopted: the metric taxonomy and the eight named runbooks (duplicate order, unprotected position, stale-data trade, venue outage, LLM outage, DB corruption, account compromise, reconciliation mismatch) with P0/P1/P2 severities (§11).

**Finding 17 (P2) — Cost estimation.** Adopted: parameterized cost model spanning LLM, data, infra, trading friction, and governance, with actual cost-per-decision and cost-per-trade logged, and friction included in economic evaluation (§12). Point ranges survive only as clearly-labeled illustrations.

**Schema corrections and revised claims table.** The `ForecastSignal` and revised `RiskVerdict` (with per-check observed value / threshold / unit / code version via `RiskCheckResult`) are adopted (§5). All ten rows of the reviewer's "claims to revise" table are addressed: SSN/tax (removed), replay "for free" (withdrawn), Hyperliquid and MetaMask claims (conditioned on the admission checklist), required LLM approval (removed), SQLite tension (resolved — below), calendar-week stages (replaced by sample-size/operational gates with calendar minimums as secondary), native-stop sufficiency (replaced by verified protection + watchdog), last-3-outcomes claim (withdrawn), cost confidence (parameterized).

## Adopted with modifications

**Finding 8 (P1) — SQLite vs. PostgreSQL.** The reviewer offered two acceptable designs and recommended Postgres as the production baseline. v2 chooses **Option A for v1**: a strict single-writer SQLite architecture (one process owns all ledger writes; everything else goes through an internal queue) with WAL, foreign keys, busy timeouts, transactional outbox, integrity checks, and a prohibition on network filesystems — with PostgreSQL designated as a hard requirement at Gate C or the moment a second writer is contemplated. Rationale: for a solo operator, the dominant failure mode is operational complexity; a correctly specified single-writer SQLite is simpler to run and back up than Postgres, and the reviewer explicitly sanctioned this design. The v1 flaw the reviewer identified — three containers sharing SQLite with unspecified concurrency — is fixed either way.

**Finding 12 (P1) — Orchestrator aggregation.** The framework is adopted: calibrated forecasts as the common unit, reliability weights with conservative shrinkage toward zero, de-duplication by feature lineage and residual correlation, shrunk covariance with stress overrides, turnover/cost penalties, round-then-recheck, and the rule that the LLM explains/vetoes but never does portfolio math. One element is simplified: instead of "solve a constrained sizing problem," v1 uses the reviewer's own alternative — **equal-risk contribution with caps and vol targeting**. A full constrained optimizer over a two-asset (later three-asset) book adds estimation-error sensitivity and code surface without meaningful benefit at this universe size; it is deferred until the universe grows enough for an optimizer to earn its complexity.

**Finding 15 (P1) — Security controls.** Most items are adopted: mounted secrets in place of broad env exposure, executor egress allowlisting, pinned and scanned dependencies, non-root read-only containers, hardened Telegram control (allowlisted IDs, confirmation for destructive commands, replay protection), redaction of secrets from prompts/logs, drilled out-of-band kill (on-chain agent-key revocation, which works even with the host compromised). Two items are consciously **deferred, not rejected**: SBOM generation and signed release artifacts with reviewed deployment commits. Rationale: proportionality — these controls defend against supply-chain and insider-deployment threats that are real but lower-ranked for a single-operator system at micro capital; they are scheduled as preconditions for raising capital beyond the micro-live tier rather than for v1. If this trade-off is unacceptable to you, they are cheap to pull forward.

**Finding 6, one sub-point — hard-halt flatten policy.** The reviewer noted both that halting-without-flattening leaves losing exposure active and that auto-flattening can crystallize losses in transient volatility, asking for explicit mode transitions. v2 specifies the transitions and picks a side for v1: **hard halt flattens**, on the reasoning that at micro size the cost of crystallizing a small loss is lower than the cost of discretionary judgment (or worse, LLM judgment) about "transient" volatility inside an incident. The predeclared policy is a config value, so this choice can be revisited with evidence.

## Not fixed (with reasons)

**Hyperliquid demoted from the narrative entirely.** The reviewer's table asks that venue selection follow only from formal review. v2 complies procedurally (the checklist gates everything) but still names Hyperliquid as the leading candidate rather than presenting a blank slate. Reason: the plan is more useful with a concrete default, and the specific credential-separation property (trade-only agent wallets, on-chain revocation) is a genuine architectural input — the out-of-band kill design in §11 depends on it. The naming is explicitly conditional.

**"Advisor team of several agents" from your initial preferences, restored gradually rather than immediately.** v2 launches with one LLM advisor (plus the deterministic baseline) instead of the 3–5 advisors of v1, per Finding 10 and the narrower-v1 recommendation. This isn't a permanent rejection of your multi-advisor design — the architecture (forecast store, calibration layer, correlation clustering) is built for N advisors — but each advisor is added one at a time under its own research contract, because each addition multiplies the multiple-testing burden from Finding 1. The four-role team you specified (advisors / orchestrator / executor / risk manager) survives intact; what changed is that the orchestrator and risk manager are now primarily deterministic engines with LLM assistants, rather than LLM agents with deterministic assistants.

No other review recommendations were declined.
